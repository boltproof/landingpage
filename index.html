The reality of data protection for small and mid-sized organisations.

Many SMEs assume that using Microsoft 365, Google Workspace or similar platforms automatically means they are ‚Äúbacked up‚Äù.

In practice, these services are designed for access and collaboration ‚Äî not for independent, offline, versioned backup or full disaster recovery.

If a file is deleted, encrypted or overwritten in a typical cloud-only setup, that change is usually synchronised everywhere. Without a separate backup, the data is often gone.

This page is purely informational. It summarises how cloud, backup, ransomware and compliance interact in real life for SMEs in Ireland, Northern Ireland and the UK. It does not constitute legal advice or formal security guidance.

At a glance
Cloud: Excellent for access, collaboration and sync.
Backup: Requires independent copies, retention and restore testing.
Compliance: Driven by GDPR, insurance wording and sector rules.

Key points: cloud sync is not the same as independent backup, and this has consequences for data loss, regulatory duties and insurance cover.

Cloud vs Backup

Cloud services are not full backups

Cloud storage (OneDrive, Google Drive, Dropbox, etc.) is primarily a synchronisation and access service. It keeps multiple devices in step and makes files easy to reach from different locations.

When files are deleted, encrypted or overwritten, those changes are normally propagated to the cloud copy as well.

Without a separate backup system, the ‚Äúcopy‚Äù in the cloud is likely to be affected in the same way as the original.

Responsibilities

Vendors expect customers to handle backup

Major cloud providers operate on a shared-responsibility model: they keep their platforms running, but customers are expected to manage their own backup, retention and recovery strategies.

This is standard, but it can be surprising for organisations who assume that ‚Äúin the cloud‚Äù automatically means ‚Äúfully protected‚Äù.

Regulation

GDPR emphasises resilience and restoration

Article 32 of the GDPR refers to the ‚Äúintegrity, availability and resilience‚Äù of systems and the ability to restore access to personal data in a timely manner after an incident.

In real-world practice, this typically requires independent backups and the ability to demonstrate that restoration is possible.

What cloud does well ‚Äì and where it stops

Cloud platforms are hugely valuable, and this page is not an argument against using them. The goal is simply to clarify what they do, and what they do not do, from a data-protection perspective.

Cloud services typically provide:

  • ‚úî File synchronisation across multiple devices
  • ‚úî Browser and mobile access from almost anywhere
  • ‚úî Collaboration features such as sharing and co-authoring

In simple terms: cloud is about working with your data. Backup and disaster recovery are about getting it back when something goes badly wrong.

A typical resilient approach (vendor-neutral)

The exact design varies, but a common pattern is to combine local backup, an encrypted off-site copy, and a separate disaster-recovery location or service.

Examples of what can go wrong with cloud-only setups

The scenarios below are simplified and anonymised, but they reflect patterns seen repeatedly in small and mid-sized organisations.

Healthcare clinic ‚Äì cloud sync and ransomware Workstations were synchronised to a cloud drive. Ransomware encrypted local files and the encrypted versions were promptly synchronised. With no independent backup, there was no clean copy to restore. Operations were severely disrupted and a great deal of time was spent rebuilding records.
Professional services firm ‚Äì insider deletion A departing staff member deleted shared folders from a cloud workspace. The deletion was only noticed months later, after standard retention had expired. Without separate backups, years of documents were lost.
Construction business ‚Äì accidental overwrites Drawings and project documents were stored entirely in a single cloud location. Overwrites and deletions during a project left the team unsure which version was correct. With no structured backup or versioning strategy, some earlier states could not be recovered.
